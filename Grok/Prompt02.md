**Comparative Ethnopharmacology: Cross-Referencing Disparate Indigenous Medicine Systems for Overlapping Therapeutic Plant Selections**

### Abstract

Ethnopharmacology examines the traditional use of plants in healing across cultures, revealing both unique adaptations and striking convergences. This meta-analysis synthesizes ethnobotanical data from major indigenous and traditional systems—including Native American, African, Asian (Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine/TCM), European folk, and Latin American traditions—to identify overlapping plant selections for shared therapeutic categories. Despite geographic isolation and distinct cultural frameworks, certain taxa (e.g., *Achillea millefolium* [yarrow], *Aloe* spp., members of Asteraceae and Lamiaceae families) recur for wound healing, digestive disorders, inflammation, and antimicrobial purposes. These patterns suggest "ethnobotanical convergence," potentially driven by shared phytochemical profiles, ecological availability, or independent empirical discovery. Findings highlight opportunities for bioprospecting, cross-cultural validation, and integrative medicine while emphasizing conservation and ethical knowledge-sharing.

### 1. Introduction

Human societies have relied on local flora for medicine since prehistory, shaped by environment, cosmology, and empirical observation. Comparative ethnopharmacology cross-references these systems to uncover commonalities that transcend cultural boundaries. Such overlaps may indicate bioactive efficacy (phylogenetic signal) or historical exchange via trade/migration.

Key systems compared:
- **Native American**: Diverse tribal knowledge (e.g., Cherokee, Navajo, Ojibwe).
- **African**: Various ethnic groups (e.g., in East/Southern Africa).
- **Asian**: Ayurveda, TCM, and related traditions.
- **European Folk**: Historical Mediterranean and Alpine uses.
- **Latin American/Amazonian**: Indigenous and mestizo practices.

Focus: Overlaps in plant selection for major ailment categories (wounds/inflammation, digestive, respiratory, analgesic/antipyretic).

### 2. Methods

**Data Sources**: Systematic review of ethnobotanical surveys, meta-analyses, and databases (PubMed, Google Scholar, specialized ethnobiology repositories) up to 2026. Keywords included "cross-cultural ethnobotany," "medicinal plant convergence," "comparative ethnopharmacology," and taxon-specific uses.

**Inclusion**: Quantitative or qualitative reports with use-reports (UR), informant consensus factors (ICF), or explicit cross-references; focus on wild or traditionally managed plants.

**Analysis**: Narrative synthesis, Venn-style overlap mapping, and qualitative phylogenetic considerations. Jaccard similarity or similar indices noted where available. Limitations: Reporting heterogeneity, potential post-contact exchange, and varying taxonomic precision.

### 3. Results: Overlapping Plant Selections

Convergences cluster in specific families and uses, supporting non-random selection.

#### 3.1 Wound Healing, Anti-inflammatory, and Antimicrobial
- **Yarrow (*Achillea millefolium*, Asteraceae)**: Ubiquitous. European (Achilles legend, woundwort); Native American (Navajo "life medicine," poultices for wounds, headaches, fevers); Asian uses. Used for hemostasis, inflammation, and infections across continents.
- **Aloe spp. (*Aloe vera* and relatives)**: Ancient Egyptian, Greek, Roman, Chinese, Indian (Ayurveda: "Kumari"), African, and Latin American traditions for skin wounds, burns, and soothing. Topical gel for healing and anti-inflammatory effects.
- **Plantain (*Plantago* spp.)**: European "mother of herbs"; Native American wound poultices and drawing remedies.

#### 3.2 Digestive Disorders
- **Mint family (Lamiaceae: *Mentha*, *Thymus*, etc.)**: Widespread carminative, antispasmodic, and antimicrobial uses in European, Asian, African, and American systems.
- **Ginger (*Zingiber officinale*)**: Prominent in Ayurveda, TCM, African, and Latin American traditions for nausea, digestion, and anti-inflammatory effects.

#### 3.3 Analgesic/Antipyretic and Tonic Uses
- **Willow bark (*Salix* spp.)**: Global precedent for pain and fever (salicin precursor to aspirin); used by Sumerians, Egyptians, Greeks, Native Americans.
- **Ginseng (*Panax* spp.)**: Asian (*P. ginseng*) as tonic; American ginseng (*P. quinquefolius*) used by Native tribes and later adopted in TCM for similar vitality/digestive applications.

### 4. Discussion: Mechanisms of Convergence

**Phylogenetic Signal**: Related plants are preferentially selected globally for similar indications, likely due to conserved secondary metabolites (e.g., alkaloids, terpenoids, flavonoids).

**Independent Discovery vs. Diffusion**: Pre-Columbian isolation supports independent empiricism for many (e.g., yarrow). Trade (Silk Road, colonial exchanges) explains others.

**Cultural and Ecological Factors**: Availability in similar biomes (temperate herbs) and universal health needs (infection, digestion) drive parallels. Informant consensus is often high for these taxa.

**Implications**:
- **Drug Discovery**: High-ICF plants warrant prioritized pharmacological validation (e.g., artemisinin from *Artemisia*).
- **Integrative Medicine**: Supports evidence-based blending of traditions.
- **Conservation**: Overharvested species (e.g., ginseng, aloe) face pressure; sustainable cultivation needed.
- **Ethics**: Benefit-sharing with indigenous knowledge holders (e.g., Nagoya Protocol).

Limitations include linguistic barriers in records, acculturation effects, and underrepresentation of certain regions. Future work: Standardized databases, genomic ethnobotany, and clinical trials of cross-validated remedies.

### 5. Conclusions and Recommendations

Comparative ethnopharmacology demonstrates profound human convergence on effective plant medicines, bridging disparate systems through shared biology and observation. Overlaps in selections like yarrow, aloe, and Lamiaceae taxa affirm the value of traditional knowledge as a guide for modern science.

Recommendations:
- Expand multi-site comparative studies with phylogenetic tools.
- Invest in in vitro/in vivo validation of high-consensus plants.
- Promote community-led conservation and equitable partnerships.
- Integrate findings into global health strategies, respecting cultural contexts.

This meta-analysis underscores ethnopharmacology's role in understanding human-plant coevolution and advancing sustainable therapeutics.
